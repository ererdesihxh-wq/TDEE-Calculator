Using your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is the most scientific approach to weight loss. Unlike fad diets that prescribe arbitrary calorie limits, a TDEE-based approach personalizes your weight loss plan to your body, activity level, and goals. This guide will show you exactly how to transform your TDEE number into a sustainable weight loss strategy.

Important: The National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases warns against very low-calorie diets (under 1,200 calories for women, 1,500 for men) without medical supervision, as they can cause nutrient deficiencies, muscle loss, and metabolic damage.

The Right Calorie Deficit: Why 15-25% is the Sweet Spot

A calorie deficit means consuming fewer calories than your body burns (TDEE). But how much of a deficit is optimal?

Small Deficit (10-15% below TDEE)

  • Best for: Those with little to lose, active individuals, or long-term lifestyle changes
  • Weight loss rate: 0.5-1 lb per week
  • Advantages: Minimal hunger, preserves muscle mass, easier to maintain

Moderate Deficit (20-25% below TDEE)

  • Best for: Most people with significant weight to lose
  • Weight loss rate: 1-2 lbs per week (CDC recommended rate)
  • Advantages: Steady progress while still sustainable

Large Deficit (30%+ below TDEE)

  • Not recommended for most people without medical supervision
  • Risks: Muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, metabolic adaptation, high relapse rate

Step-by-Step: Calculate Your Weight Loss Calories

1 Calculate Your TDEE

Use our TDEE Calculator to get your personalized Total Daily Energy Expenditure. For example, let's say your TDEE is 2,400 calories.

2 Apply the Appropriate Deficit

For moderate weight loss (20% deficit):
2,400 calories × 0.80 = 1,920 calories per day

This creates a daily deficit of 480 calories (2,400 - 1,920), which equals about 1 lb of fat loss per week (480 × 7 = 3,360 calories ≈ 1 lb of fat).

3 Set Your Protein Target

Protein is crucial for preserving muscle during weight loss. The International Society of Sports Nutrition recommends 1.6-2.2 grams of protein per kg of body weight for those in a calorie deficit.

Example: For a 180 lb (82 kg) person:
82 kg × 2.0 g/kg = 164 grams of protein daily
At 4 calories per gram: 164 × 4 = 656 calories from protein

4 Distribute Remaining Calories

After protein, allocate remaining calories between fats and carbohydrates based on preference:

  • Fats: 20-30% of total calories (0.5-1g per kg)
  • Carbs: Remainder of calories

Complete Weight Loss Macro Example

Person: 35-year-old female, 160 lbs, TDEE = 2,150 calories

Weight Loss Target: 1,720 calories (20% deficit)

Macros:

  • Protein: 73 kg × 1.8 g/kg = 131g (524 calories)
  • Fat: 25% of calories = 48g (432 calories)
  • Carbs: Remaining calories = 191g (764 calories)

Daily Totals: 1,720 calories | 131g P | 48g F | 191g C

5 Common Weight Loss Mistakes to Avoid

1. Creating Too Large a Deficit

Aggressive restriction triggers adaptive thermogenesis—your metabolism slows to conserve energy. A 2012 study found that contestants from "The Biggest Loser" experienced metabolic slowing of up to 800 calories per day, making weight regain likely.

2. Not Tracking Accurately

Research shows people underestimate food intake by 20-50%. Use a food scale for the first few weeks to calibrate your eye.

3. Neglecting Resistance Training

Cardio burns calories but resistance training preserves muscle—and muscle burns more calories at rest. Include 2-3 strength sessions weekly.

4. Ignoring Non-Scale Victories

Scale weight fluctuates daily. Track measurements, photos, clothing fit, and energy levels too.

5. Not Planning for Maintenance

What happens when you reach your goal? Reverse dieting (gradually increasing calories) helps reset your metabolism.

When and How to Adjust Your Weight Loss Plan

Your TDEE decreases as you lose weight. Every 10-15 lbs lost, recalculate your TDEE and adjust your calorie target accordingly.

Signs you need to adjust:

  • Weight plateau for 3+ weeks despite consistency
  • Excessive hunger or fatigue
  • Loss of menstrual cycle (women)
  • Noticeable muscle loss or strength decline

Pro Tip: Implement a "diet break" every 8-12 weeks—eat at maintenance calories for 1-2 weeks. This can help normalize hormone levels, boost metabolism, and improve adherence to your plan.

Breaking Through Weight Loss Plateaus

If you've hit a plateau despite a consistent deficit:

  1. Reassess your TDEE: You may need a smaller deficit now
  2. Increase NEAT: Add 2,000-3,000 extra steps daily
  3. Try protein cycling: Higher protein days (2.4g/kg) followed by moderate days
  4. Change training stimulus: Swap steady-state cardio for HIIT or increase weights
  5. Check for hidden calories: Oils, dressings, beverages, or portion creep
